## The Question That Started It All

"Does investing in partner training actually drive revenue?"

It sounds like a simple question. My team was working at a large tech company that manages an extensive partner ecosystem — thousands of partners across multiple geographic regions, spanning several solution areas. Every year, the company pours millions into partner skilling programs: certifications, training courses, role-based competency tracks. The assumption had always been intuitive — train partners, they sell better, revenue goes up.

But when leadership asked us to *quantify* that assumption, we realized we didn't actually know if it was true. We had the data: monthly training metrics (certifications earned, people trained) and revenue figures for each partner, broken down by region and solution area. Multiple fiscal years of data. Hundreds of thousands of potential data points.

The assignment seemed straightforward: build a model, find the relationship, give leadership a number they can plan around. "If we increase training by X%, revenue goes up by Y%."

What followed was a six-month journey through progressively more sophisticated modeling approaches, a fundamental rethinking of what our models were actually measuring, and a lesson in why the most important thing in data science isn't your algorithm — it's your question.

This is the story of how we went from correlation to causation, from XGBoost to Double Machine Learning, and from "looks good on a slide" to "actually useful for decision-making."

---

## Act I: The Obvious Approach (That Turned Out to Be Wrong)

### Starting with Linear Regression

Like any sensible team with a regression problem, we started simple. Ordinary Least Squares. Two independent variables (certifications and trained learners), one dependent variable (revenue). We worked in log-space because the revenue distributions were heavily right-skewed — a few massive partners dominate the raw numbers.

```python
import numpy as np
import statsmodels.api as sm

# Log-transform to handle skew
X = np.log1p(df[['cert_count', 'learner_count']])
X = sm.add_constant(X)
y = np.log1p(df['revenue'])

model = sm.OLS(y, X).fit()
print(model.summary())
```

R² came back at about 0.30. Not terrible for noisy real-world business data. The coefficients were positive — more certifications and more training correlated with higher revenue. The p-values were significant.

We presented preliminary results. People nodded. "Makes sense," they said.

But something nagged at me.

### Throwing More Compute at It

When in doubt, try gradient boosting. That's basically a rule at this point, right?

We ran the usual suspects: Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM. Hyperparameter tuning. Cross-validation. The works.

```python
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestRegressor
from xgboost import XGBRegressor
from lightgbm import LGBMRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import r2_score

models = {
    'Random Forest': RandomForestRegressor(n_estimators=200, random_state=42),
    'XGBoost': XGBRegressor(n_estimators=200, learning_rate=0.1, random_state=42),
    'LightGBM': LGBMRegressor(n_estimators=200, random_state=42)
}

for name, model in models.items():
    model.fit(X_train, y_train)
    preds = model.predict(X_test)
    print(f"{name}: R² = {r2_score(y_test, preds):.3f}")
```

Results:

| Model | R² |
|-------|-----|
| OLS (log-space) | 0.30 |
| Random Forest | 0.40 |
| XGBoost | 0.38 |
| LightGBM | 0.39 |

Better. Marginal improvement. Feature importance plots confirmed what we expected: trained learners mattered more than certifications. We could generate nice SHAP plots. The models looked reasonable.

But here's the thing — we weren't actually answering the *question*.

Leadership didn't ask "can you predict which partners will have high revenue?" They asked "does *training cause* higher revenue, and by *how much*?"

And that's a fundamentally different question than anything a predictive model can answer.

## Act II: The Confounder Problem (Or, Why I Should Have Paid More Attention in Statistics Class)

### The Ice Cream and Drowning Problem

There's a classic example in introductory statistics: ice cream sales and drowning deaths are highly correlated. When ice cream sales go up, drowning deaths go up. Does ice cream cause drowning?

Obviously not. Summer causes both. Hot weather drives people to buy ice cream *and* to go swimming (where some, tragically, drown). Summer is a **confounder** — a variable that independently causes both the thing you're measuring and the thing you're trying to predict.

Here's another one: the number of firefighters at a fire is positively correlated with the amount of damage. More firefighters, more damage. Should we send fewer firefighters to reduce damage? Of course not — larger fires cause both more damage *and* more firefighters to be deployed. Fire size is the confounder.

These examples sound silly because the confounders are obvious. But in business data, confounders hide in plain sight.

### Our Confounder: Partner Size

Once we started looking for it, the confounder in our data was painfully obvious.

**Large partners** naturally have:
- More employees → more people available to train → higher training numbers
- More market presence → more deals in pipeline → higher revenue
- More resources → more capacity for certifications → higher certification counts

Partner size was driving *both* training metrics *and* revenue. Our regression model was picking up the size signal and calling it a "training effect."

Think about it concretely. Take two real (anonymized) partners from our dataset:

| Partner | Size | Certifications | Trained Learners | Revenue |
|---------|------|---------------|-----------------|---------|
| Partner A | 5,000+ employees | 800 | 1,200 | $85M |
| Partner B | 250 employees | 120 | 180 | $7M |

The naive regression sees these two data points and concludes: "Training drives revenue! Look at that beautiful positive correlation!" But Partner A's $85M revenue comes from its massive sales organization, decade-long client relationships, and market positioning — not primarily from its training numbers.

If we used our naive regression to tell Partner B "just train 1,200 people and you'll hit $85M," we'd be giving catastrophically bad advice. That partner simply doesn't have the market presence, sales force, or client base to generate $85M — no matter how many certifications their team earns.

And this wasn't a theoretical concern. **We were about to feed these coefficients into an optimization engine that would generate specific training recommendations for each partner.** If the coefficients were wrong — inflated by confounders — every recommendation downstream would be wrong too.

### Why Tree Models Can't Fix This

I want to be really clear about something that took me embarrassingly long to internalize: **XGBoost, Random Forest, and LightGBM cannot solve the confounder problem.**

Here's why. These models are optimized to predict. They find patterns in data — any patterns — and use them to minimize prediction error. They don't distinguish between:

1. **"Training directly drives revenue"** (causal signal)
2. **"Large partners happen to have both high training and high revenue"** (confounded signal)

Both patterns reduce prediction error equally well. The model doesn't care *why* the pattern exists — it just uses whatever works. A well-tuned XGBoost model will happily exploit the partner-size confound to improve its R² score.

**The key insight**: Predictive accuracy and causal understanding are not the same thing. You can have a model with R² = 0.95 that gives you completely wrong causal coefficients. And you can have a causal model with R² = 0.25 that gives you exactly the right number for decision-making.

This is the insight that changed everything for our team.

## Act III: Enter Double Machine Learning

### The Residualization Trick

Double Machine Learning (DML), developed by Chernozhukov et al. in 2017, is one of those ideas that feels obvious in hindsight but is genuinely profound.

The core idea: **if confounders are polluting both your treatment and your outcome, use machine learning to strip out the confounder effects first, then estimate the causal relationship on what's left over.**

What's "left over" after you remove confounder effects? The *residuals*. And the beautiful thing about residuals is that they contain exactly the variation that confounders *can't explain* — which is exactly where the causal signal lives.

Let me walk through the two stages:

### Stage 1: Remove confounders from both sides

**Step 1a — Model the outcome.** Train a machine learning model to predict revenue (Y) using *only* the confounders (X) — partner size, geographic region, solution area, partner segment. Deliberately ignore the treatment variable (training metrics).

```
Revenue_predicted = ML_model_1(partner_size, region, segment, ...)
Revenue_residual  = Actual_Revenue - Revenue_predicted
```

The revenue residual is the part of revenue that partner size, geography, and segment *cannot explain*. It's the "surprise" revenue — the *extra* revenue (or shortfall) that the partner has *beyond what you'd expect* given their size and market.

**Step 1b — Model the treatment.** Train a *separate* ML model to predict training metrics (T) using the same confounders (X).

```
Training_predicted = ML_model_2(partner_size, region, segment, ...)
Training_residual  = Actual_Training - Training_predicted
```

The training residual is the "surprise" training — how much *more or less* training the partner did *beyond what you'd expect* given their size.

### Stage 2: Find the causal relationship in the residuals

Now do a simple regression of the revenue residuals on the training residuals:

```
Revenue_residual = θ × Training_residual + noise
```

**θ is your causal coefficient.**

Why does this work? Because both residuals have had the confounder effects surgically removed. The revenue residual doesn't contain partner-size effects. The training residual doesn't contain partner-size effects. So when you regress one on the other, the only relationship left is the *direct effect* of "surprise training" on "surprise revenue."

It's called "**Double**" Machine Learning because you use ML twice — once to model the outcome, once to model the treatment. Both times, the goal is the same: partial out confounders.

### Implementation: It's Surprisingly Clean

The EconML library (open source) makes this straightforward:

```python
from econml.dml import LinearDML
from sklearn.linear_model import LassoCV

# Define the variables
confounders = df[['partner_size', 'region_encoded', 'segment_encoded',
                   'solution_area_encoded', 'period_encoded']]
treatment = df[['cert_count', 'learner_count']]
outcome = df['revenue']

# Fit the DML model
dml = LinearDML(
    model_y=LassoCV(),   # Stage 1a: predict revenue from confounders
    model_t=LassoCV(),   # Stage 1b: predict training from confounders
    random_state=42
)
dml.fit(Y=outcome, T=treatment, X=confounders)

# The causal coefficients
print("Causal effects:", dml.coef_)
print("95% CI:", dml.coef__interval(alpha=0.05))
```

That's it. Three classes, a few lines of code, and you go from confounded correlation to deconfounded causation.

## Act IV: What Changed When We Switched

### The Numbers Got Smaller (And That's a Good Thing)

Here's the comparison that I still think about:

| Approach | Cert Effect on Revenue | Learner Effect on Revenue |
|----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| OLS | +$2.4M per 100 certs | +$1.8M per 100 learners |
| XGBoost (SHAP) | ~+$2.1M | ~+$1.5M |
| LinearDML | +$0.4M per 100 certs | +$0.6M per 100 learners |

The DML estimates are roughly **one-fifth** the size of the OLS estimates. And that's exactly what we expected — because the OLS numbers were inflated by the partner-size confound. The OLS model was attributing revenue that was *actually caused by partner size* to training.

The DML numbers are smaller, but they're *real*. They represent the actual causal impact of training, stripped of confounding effects.

### Better Confidence Intervals

DML gives you proper confidence intervals for the causal effects. Our OLS confidence intervals were misleadingly narrow because the model was overfitting to the confounded signal. The DML intervals were wider but honest — they reflected the true uncertainty in our causal estimates.

### Downstream Impact: The Optimization Engine

Remember, these coefficients feed into an optimization engine (Pyomo/IPOPT) that generates specific training recommendations. With the inflated OLS coefficients, the optimizer was generating conservative training targets — because each training unit appeared to have a huge impact, you didn't need many. With the correct DML coefficients, the optimizer generated more realistic (and larger) training targets that actually reflected the effort needed to move revenue.

## Key Takeaways

If you're working with observational business data and trying to understand *what drives what*, here's what I'd recommend:

1. **Start with the question, not the model.** Are you trying to *predict* or *explain*? These require fundamentally different approaches.
2. **Be paranoid about confounders.** If your treatment and outcome share a common cause, your regression coefficients are wrong. Full stop.
3. **Tree models won't save you.** XGBoost is excellent for prediction. It tells you nothing reliable about causation.
4. **DML is remarkably accessible.** The EconML library makes causal inference available to anyone who can write scikit-learn code.
5. **Smaller coefficients can be better.** If your causal estimates drop significantly when you account for confounders, that's not a failure — it's your analysis working correctly.
6. **Causal estimates feed better decisions.** If your coefficients feed into planning, optimization, or budget allocation, you need causal estimates, not correlations.

The LLM is a powerful tool. But like any powerful tool, it demands respect — and a healthy dose of skepticism about what your models are actually telling you.
